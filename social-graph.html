When you correlate all three leaked files, you find a clear fingerprint: all of them share the same machine SID in their binary metadata — the Virtual Station 04 worker node — and all final saves occurred within the same two-hour window on March 18th, while all five users were logged in at different times.

Someone stayed behind and exfiltrated the files from the sandbox.

At this point, your director authorizes legally sanctioned surveillance.

Examples of legally sanctioned surveillance in real countries

  • UK Investigatory Powers Act (2016) allows bulk metadata collection.
  • France’s 2015 Surveillance Law permits intelligence agencies to monitor communications linked to terrorism or espionage.
  • Switzerland’s NDG (Nachrichtendienstgesetz, 2017) expanded state monitoring capabilities with parliamentary oversight.

Social graph from metadata

You start gathering metadata — not contents — from phone calls, emails, office Wi-Fi logs, social media, and VPN endpoints, in hopes to find incriminating information on the members of the lab. Using this information you build a social graph that you hope will give you insight into what’s going on.

A social graph built from call or message metadata connects people (nodes) when they communicate (edges). You rarely see content—only who contacted whom, how often, and when.

What is an “insider” here?

An insider is someone positioned to move sensitive data out of a trusted enclave. In graph terms, look for a node that is both highly connected and sits on many weighted paths—a hub or broker—not necessarily the loudest speaker.

Success criteria

  • Compute weighted degree (sum of edge weights) per person.
  • Use Highlight hubs to compare the strongest connectors.
  • Select the person who best matches the broker pattern, then confirm.

Real-world context: large-scale communication metadata has been central to debates about programs that collect “who talked to whom” at population scale.
