THE LEAK

This is an educational blog, meant to teach graduate Computer Science students about cyber‑surveillance and inference of private information via metadata analysis through an interactive investigation scenario.

Motivation

Since the early 2000s, cyber-espionage has become the weapon of choice for national security organizations worldwide. Intelligence agencies — from the NSA to GCHQ to Switzerland’s own NDB — have conducted large-scale digital surveillance operations, often in the name of national security.

In 2013, the Edward Snowden revelations leaked, exposing the global reach of programs like PRISM and XKeyscore: intelligence programs that enabled near-omniscient mass surveillance of emails, browsing habits, and online activity across the globe. This shook public trust in governments’ ability to manage digital personal data responsibly and protect citizen's privacy.

Many people assume that encrypting our communications is enough to guarantee privacy. However, as this blog will show, encryption alone often does not provide full privacy protection when using modern communication systems.

Government-led surveillance programs frequently rely on the observation of communications metadata — rather than message content — to infer sensitive information about individuals.

What is metadata?

Metadata is data about data: who created a file, when it was edited, which device or app produced it, where it was saved, and how it moved between systems (for example IP routes or message headers).

Even when the content is encrypted or redacted, metadata can still describe behavior, relationships, and routines. That is why it is powerful for investigators—and for anyone building profiles at scale.

You will take the role of an agent at the Global Liaison in Threat Tracking and Espionage Response Agency of the fictional Republic of Auralia (also known as G.L.I.T.T.E.R.).

Your task in this simulation is to investigate a data breach that threatens to expose classified information on state-led surveillance programs.

What you will do

  1. Look at document metadata (like who created and edited a file) and use them to build a timeline that points to the main suspect.
  2. Study a network of messages and calls to find the person who connects and passes information between agents and journalists.
  3. Use location data to guess where someone lives, works, and where they stop at a sensitive place.
  4. Take part in a debate about security versus privacy.
