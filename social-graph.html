When you correlate all three leaked files, you find a clear fingerprint: all of them share the same machine SID in their binary metadata — the Virtual Station 04 worker node — and all final saves occurred within the same two-hour window on March 18th, while all five users were logged in at different times.

Someone stayed behind and exfiltrated the files from the sandbox.

At this point, your director authorizes legally sanctioned surveillance.

Examples of legally sanctioned surveillance in real countries

  • UK Investigatory Powers Act (2016) allows bulk metadata collection.
  • France’s 2015 Surveillance Law permits intelligence agencies to monitor communications linked to terrorism or espionage.
  • Switzerland’s NDG (Nachrichtendienstgesetz, 2017) expanded state monitoring capabilities with parliamentary oversight.

Social graph from metadata

You start gathering metadata from encrypted phone calls and emails that you intercepted using your super cool spy tools, in hopes to find incriminating information on the members of the lab. Using this information you build a social graph that you hope will give you insight into what’s going on.

A social graph built from call or message metadata connects people (nodes) when they communicate . You don't need to see what the people where talking about, just who contacted whom, how often, and when to infer useful information.

What is an “insider” here?

An insider is someone positioned to move sensitive data out of a supposedly private network. In graph terms, look for a node that is both highly connected to the nodes of other agency workers, and to suspicious externl actors such as journalists Cady Perry, Michael Johnson or Ma Donna.

Real-world parallels

  • The NSA collected vast amounts of phone call metadata—records of who called whom, when, and for how long—from U.S. telecommunications providers under Section 215 of the Patriot Act, enabling it to build detailed social‑network profiles on millions of ordinary citizens without ever listening to the actual content of the conversations.
