Real-world anchors

Edward Snowden’s 2013 disclosures to journalists catalyzed global debate about NSA programs that relied heavily on metadata (e.g., call records and network selectors). Separately, location metadata from apps like Strava has highlighted military and personal security risks when routes are aggregated on public maps.

Since Edward Snowden’s 2013 disclosures, the world of surveillance has become both more visible and more entrenched. His leaks exposed the scale of bulk metadata collection, uprooted the “security vs. privacy” myth, and triggered modest reforms like the USA FREEDOM Act and a push toward encrypted web traffic. At the same time, states have expanded digital‑surveillance powers, embraced lawful‑interception‑style architectures, and leaned further on data‑sharing with tech platforms, deepening the reach of always‑on monitoring. Public awareness and skepticism have grown, but the underlying infrastructure—of metadata‑driven analytics, location‑tracking, and cross‑agency data sharing—is now more pervasive than before, making surveillance less an exception and more the default backdrop of digital life.
